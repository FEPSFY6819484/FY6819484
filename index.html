Abstract

A Formula 1 cars aerodynamics can decide whether it stays on the track or takes a chance on taking off at speeds exceeding 200mph. Increasing a car's speed and efficiency has always been largely dependent on aerodynamics. Weather thatâ€™s the cigar-shaped models of the early days or complex, wind-tunnel-tested marvels of today, the development of aerodynamic innovation has transformed racing. This essay focuses on how aerodynamics in Formula 1 has evolved from very humble beginnings to its current position as a key shaper of performance, safety, and sustainability.

Early Formula 1 cars had a straightforward cigar-shaped design that prioritized mechanical functionality and aesthetics over aerodynamic performance. (F1Technical, n.d.). These cars' exposed wheels and rounded noses produced significant aerodynamic drag. Because engines had to exert more effort to overcome drag forces, air resistance increased. The car's airflow was turbulent due to its streamlined bodywork. These early cars' flat undersides produced lift rather than downforce by acting as inverted wings. This lift decreased tire contact with the track at high speeds, impairing stability and grip. Drivers found it difficult to maintain control, particularly when braking and cornering, raising the possibility of collisions. (LAS Motorsport, n.d.). Cigar-shaped cars had no means of producing downforce because they lacked aerodynamic aids like wings or diffusers. This absence limited cornering speeds and overall handling capabilities, as the cars relied solely on mechanical grip from tires and suspension systems. (Formula1.com, n.d.).
